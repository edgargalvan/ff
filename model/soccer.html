4 MODEL

Table below is a list of basic assumptions made in the current model.

Parameter Property
Point scored \(y_{gi}\) Observable
Scoring intensity \(\theta_{gi}\) Unobservable
Attacking and defensive strengths Unovservable
Expected Performance Unit of performance
Distribution of \(\theta_{gi}\) ~Poisson
Distribution of $ygi | θgi $ ~Poisson

The observed counts (score) \(y = (y_{g1},y_{g2})\) is modeled as independent Poison

\begin{equation} y_{gj} \vert \theta_{gj} = Poisson(\theta_{gj})\\ \end{equation}

where the indices correspond to the \(g\)-th game and the home (\(j=1\)) and away (\(j=2\)) teams, respectively. Assume a log-linear random effect model (a common model but this should really be validated in some way).

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} & \log{\theta_{g1}} = intercept + home + att_{h(g)} + def_{a(g)} \\ & \log{\theta_{g2}} = intercept + att_{a(g)} + def_{h(g)} \end{aligned} \end{equation}

Where \(intercept\) is the standard intercept parameter, which in this context represents the average goals scored per game by the away team; \(home\) is the home advantage parameter. The home advantage is assumed to be consistent for all teams and throughout the season. The scoring intensity, \(\theta\) is determined by the attack (offense) and defensive strengths of the teams. Note that a negative defense parameter will weaken opposing teams attacking parameter. The subscripts \(h\) and \(a\) are for home and away, respectively [TODO:aren’t these indices inconsistent]. The model data structure consists of the game week and id, home and away teams, and total points scored. We Assume the flat priors

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} home & \sim Normal(0,0.0001)\\ intercept & \sim Normal(0,0.001) \end{aligned} \end{equation} 
with pm.Model() as model:
    home = pm.Normal('home', 0, .0001, testval=0)
    intercept = pm.Normal('intercept', 0, .0001, testval=0)

The hyper-prior (priors on hierarchical model parameter ) are assumed

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \tau_{att} & \sim Normal(0.1, 0.1)\\ \tau_{def} & \sim Normal(0.1, 0.1) \end{aligned} \end{equation} 
with model:
    tau_att = pm.Gamma('tau_att', .1, .1, testval=10)
    tau_def = pm.Gamma('tau_def', .1, .1, testval=10)

The team strengths are modeled ad

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \att_{t} & \sim Normal(0, \tau_{att})\\ \def_{t} & \sim Normal(0, \tau+{def}) \end{aligned} \end{equation} 
#team-specific parameters
with model:
    atts_star = pm.Normal("atts_star",
			    mu=0,
			    tau=tau_att,
			    shape=num_teams,
			    testval=att_starting_points.values)

    defs_star = pm.Normal("defs_star",
			    mu=0,
			    tau=tau_def,
			    shape=num_teams,
			    testval=def_starting_points.values)

The Biangiardo paper has more model parameters but they seem to over-constrain the problem. To ensure identifiability (that no two parameterizations are observationally equivalent), a sum-to-zero constraint (could have use anything here) on attack and defense parameters is assumed

\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \sum_{t=1}^{T}att_t & = 0 \\ \sum_{t=1}^{T}def_t & = 0 \\ \end{aligned} \end{equation}

Trick to code the sum to zero constraint 

with model:
	atts = pm.Deterministic("atts",atts_star.copy() - tt.mean(atts_star))
	defs = pm.Deterministic("defs",defs_star.copy() - tt.mean(defs_star))

Now, we define our remaining parameters 

with model:
    home_theta = pm.Deterministic('home_theta',intercept + home + atts[home_team] + defs[away_team])
    away_theta = pm.Deterministic('away_theta',intercept + atts[away_team] + defs[home_team])

    home_goals = pm.Poisson('home_goals',
			      mu=home_theta,
			      testval=observed_home_goals,
			      observed=True)

    away_goals = pm.Poisson('away_goals',
			      mu=away_theta,
			      testval=observed_away_goals,
			      observed=True)
